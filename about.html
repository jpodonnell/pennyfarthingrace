The history of the high wheel bicycle and the story behind the race

About the Race

The Ohio High Wheel Race is a one-hour penny farthing bicycle race held in the heart of downtown Plain City, Ohio. Riders compete in a race-to-zero format — completing as many laps of the ~0.9 mile course as possible before the clock runs out.

This inaugural race is held in conjunction with two landmark celebrations — the Plain City Corn Carnival and the 250th anniversary of the United States of America. There is no better time to revive a piece of American cycling history than during the nation's semiquincentennial celebration.

The Ohio High Wheel Race draws inspiration from the National Clustered Highwheel Race, one of the premier high wheel racing events in the country. Their passion for keeping the sport of penny farthing racing alive is what sparked the idea to bring this unique event to Plain City.

History of the Penny Farthing

The penny farthing — also known as the high wheel, high wheeler, or ordinary bicycle — is one of the most iconic and recognizable inventions of the 19th century. With its enormous front wheel and tiny rear wheel, it stands as a symbol of the early days of cycling and human-powered transportation.

Origins

The penny farthing evolved from the velocipede, an early pedal-powered bicycle sometimes called the "boneshaker" for its rough ride on cobblestone streets. In the late 1860s, French inventor Eugène Meyer and British engineer James Starley independently began developing bicycles with larger front wheels — the bigger the wheel, the farther the rider traveled with each pedal stroke, since the pedals were attached directly to the front hub with no chain or gearing.

By the 1870s, the high wheel bicycle had taken hold across Europe and North America. The name "penny farthing" came from a British comparison: the large front wheel resembled a penny coin, and the tiny rear wheel resembled a much smaller farthing coin placed side by side.

The Golden Age

The 1870s and 1880s were the golden age of the penny farthing. Racing became a popular sport, with competitions held on velodromes and closed road circuits across Britain, Europe, and the United States. Skilled riders could reach speeds exceeding 20 miles per hour — remarkable for the era.

The high wheel bicycle was also a status symbol. Owning and riding one required athleticism, balance, and a degree of daring — mounting and dismounting alone was a skill unto itself, requiring the rider to step up onto a peg above the rear wheel and leap onto the saddle while the bicycle was in motion.

The Decline

The penny farthing's reign was short-lived. By the late 1880s, John Kemp Starley (nephew of James Starley) introduced the "safety bicycle" — a chain-driven bicycle with two equal-sized wheels. It was safer, easier to ride, and accessible to a much wider range of people including women. Within a few years, the safety bicycle had all but replaced the high wheel, and the penny farthing faded from everyday use.

The Revival

While the penny farthing disappeared from roads and velodromes, it never disappeared from the hearts of cycling enthusiasts. Today, a dedicated global community of high wheel riders keeps the tradition alive — building, restoring, and racing penny farthings at events around the world.

Modern high wheel races celebrate both the history of cycling and the sheer joy of riding one of the most unusual machines ever built. The Ohio High Wheel Race is proud to be part of that tradition, bringing penny farthing racing to the streets of Plain City for the first time.
